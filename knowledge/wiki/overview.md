# MLS Overview — Synthesized from Scraped Sources

This page synthesizes what the 18 scraped files in `knowledge/raw/` tell us about Major League Soccer's structure, recruitment dynamics, current landscape, and analytical coverage. Where a topic is referenced in source URLs or navigation menus but the actual content was not captured, that gap is called out explicitly rather than filled in from outside knowledge.

## What MLS is, structurally

Major League Soccer is "the top-flight professional soccer league in the United States and Canada, and was founded in 1996 after the USA hosted the 1994 FIFA World Cup" (source: `about.md`). The league office is headquartered at PENN 2, 2 Pennsylvania Plaza, Floor 15, New York, NY 10018 (source: `about.md`). FBref classifies MLS as a 1st-tier men's competition with the United States as governing country (source: `en_comps_22_Major-League-Soccer-Stats.md`).

The league is organized into Eastern and Western Conferences. The 2026 Eastern Conference table on FBref enumerates 15 teams (Nashville SC, New England Revolution, Inter Miami, Chicago Fire, FC Cincinnati, Charlotte FC, Toronto FC, NYCFC, Columbus Crew, D.C. United, NY Red Bulls, Atlanta United, Orlando City, CF Montréal, Philadelphia Union — source: `en_comps_22_Major-League-Soccer-Stats.md`). Western Conference team names are referenced indirectly through match-result links on the Eastern Conference page (Vancouver Whitecaps, Real Salt Lake, Colorado Rapids, LA Galaxy, Minnesota United, FC Dallas, Sporting Kansas City, San Jose Earthquakes, St. Louis CITY SC, San Diego FC, Houston Dynamo, LAFC, Austin FC) but the scraped page does not include a complete Western Conference table within the first 150 lines we read; full team count for the Western Conference cannot be confirmed from the scraped content alone.

The competitive structure includes a regular season followed by the **Audi MLS Cup Playoffs**. The most recently completed edition was the **Audi 2025 MLS Cup Playoffs**, won by **Inter Miami** over **Vancouver Whitecaps** in the MLS Cup final (source: `competitions_mls-cup-playoffs.md`, headlines "Ref Cam! Inter Miami down Vancouver Whitecaps in MLS Cup", "Inter Miami bring home MLS Cup!", "Javier Mascherano guides Inter Miami to MLS Cup in first season"). The 2026 playoffs schedule has already been announced ("Audi 2026 MLS Cup Playoffs: Key dates & schedule information" — same source). The exact playoff bracket format (round structure, single vs. multi-leg, seeding rules) is not detailed in the scraped pages; the page links to a "Playoff Format Explained" section but the underlying explanation was not captured in our scrape. Other competitions visible at the season level on FBref include the **Lamar Hunt U.S. Open Cup**, the **2026 MLS Draft**, and (for women's soccer) the **NWSL** and **NWSL Challenge Cup** (source: `en_comps_22_Major-League-Soccer-Stats.md`).

## Key competitive dynamics that affect recruitment

The mlssoccer.com/about navigation (source: `about.md`) lists "Roster Rules & Regulations" and "Competition Guidelines" as official topics, and the American Soccer Analysis URLs include slugs for `breaking-down-mls-roster-rules` and `state-of-the-mls-academy-system` (sources: `home_2024_2_16_breaking-down-mls-roster-rules.md`, `home_2024_8_27_state-of-the-mls-academy-system.md`). These tell us the topics exist as a named area of analysis. **However, the actual article bodies for the ASA roster-rules and academy pieces were not captured by Firecrawl** (the scraper returned the ASA homepage for those URLs instead). As a result, the scraped content does not contain detailed descriptions of designated-player slots, allocation-money categories (GAM/TAM), the U22 initiative, or the salary cap mechanics — those topics are only attested to *as topics that exist*, not as substantive content this knowledge base can answer questions about.

What the sources *do* show concretely is that the league actively transacts in mid-season:

- The mlssoccer.com news landing page hosts a "Transfer Tracker" section with recent signings such as Real Salt Lake signing Noel Caliskan, NYCFC signing forward Arnau Farnós, Columbus Crew extending Dylan Chambost, Charlotte FC signing forward Rodolfo Aloko, Houston Dynamo signing **homegrown** Mattheo Dimareli, and Charlotte FC signing **homegrown** midfielder Aron John (source: `news.md`). The "homegrown" label evidences that an MLS-specific homegrown-player rule exists in practice, even though its formal definition was not captured.
- FBref's MLS overview page links to a dedicated **"Squad & Player Wages"** section sourced via Capology (source: `en_comps_22_Major-League-Soccer-Stats.md`), and Lionel Messi's individual page reports his Inter Miami contract as "$12,000,000 Annual, Expires December 2028" via the same Capology integration (source: `en_players_d70ce98e_Lionel-Messi.md`). Wages are therefore a publicly-traceable input to recruitment analysis.
- FBref also exposes a **"2026 MLS Draft"** page (source: `en_comps_22_Major-League-Soccer-Stats.md` — link only, page itself not in this scrape), confirming that an annual draft is part of the talent-acquisition mechanism.

## The current league landscape

**Inter Miami is the central recruitment story** in the scraped corpus. The 2025 MLS Cup playoff coverage (source: `competitions_mls-cup-playoffs.md`) features the headlines "Busquets, De Paul & Messi: Inter Miami's World Cup champions make MLS history" and "Javier Mascherano guides Inter Miami to MLS Cup in first season." Lionel Messi's FBref profile (source: `en_players_d70ce98e_Lionel-Messi.md`) confirms his current club as Inter Miami, his 2022 World Cup Champion honor, and 2026-season stats of 8 goals and 1 assist in 10 MLS appearances plus 1 goal in 2 CONCACAF Champions Cup appearances. The 2026 Eastern Conference table (source: `en_comps_22_Major-League-Soccer-Stats.md`) shows Inter Miami in 3rd place with 19 points from 11 matches, with Messi as the team's top scorer at 8 goals.

**Top of the league (early 2026)**: Nashville SC leads the East with 23 points (Sam Surridge top scorer, 9 goals), followed by New England Revolution and Inter Miami tied at 19 (source: `en_comps_22_Major-League-Soccer-Stats.md`).

**League-wide stat leaders (2026 partial season)** as reported by FBref across all of its category pages:
- **Most Goals**: Petar Musa (FC Dallas) and Hugo Cuypers (Chicago Fire), 10 each
- **Most Assists**: Son Heung-min (Los Angeles FC), 7
- **Most Clean Sheets**: Hugo Lloris (Los Angeles FC), 8

(These three lines appear identically across `en_comps_22_*` files because FBref repeats them as page banners on every category view.)

**Other prominent international names visible in scraped data**: Lionel Messi (Inter Miami, FW-MF, Argentina, age 38), Son Heung-min and Hugo Lloris (both at LAFC, formerly of Tottenham). Sergio Busquets is named alongside Messi and De Paul as part of Inter Miami's "World Cup champions" trio in the 2025 MLS Cup coverage (`competitions_mls-cup-playoffs.md`), but **his FBref profile failed to scrape (HTTP 500), so we have no detailed profile data for him**. The file `en_players_8b04d6c1_Jordi-Alba.md` is named for Jordi Alba but its content is actually Pierre-Emile Højbjerg's profile (Marseille / Denmark) — the 8-char player ID in the URL is wrong, so **we have no Jordi Alba data either** despite his presumed presence on Inter Miami.

## How analytical sites view MLS recruitment

**FBref (`fbref.com`)** is the statistical reference layer. For the 2026 MLS season alone it provides at least seven distinct category pages (sources: the seven `en_comps_22_*` files): Standard Stats, Shooting, Passing, Defensive Actions, Possession, Goalkeeping, Miscellaneous, plus a Playing Time view. Each player is identified by a stable 8-character ID (e.g., `d70ce98e` for Messi) and has a profile that includes biographical data (DOB, height, weight, footedness, nationality), career honors, season-by-season match logs going back to the player's first season, goal logs, wage data via Capology, and player-comparison tools (sources: `en_players_d70ce98e_Lionel-Messi.md`). The MLS overview also links out to nationality breakdowns, the MLS Draft, and historical season archives.

**American Soccer Analysis (`americansocceranalysis.com`)** is positioned as an independent analytics publication. From the scraped homepage (sources: `home.md` and the three article files that returned the same homepage content) we can see that ASA: (a) publishes feature articles attributed to bylined writers including Ben Bellman, Joe Lowery, and Eliot McKinley; (b) runs a Patreon-supported model at $5/month for access to "data visualization tools we use to make these previews"; (c) operates a "firewalled consulting arm" through which Mike Imburgio works with MLS clubs (the editor's note specifically mentions Minnesota United); (d) partners with the Expected Own Goals podcast for audio coverage. The homepage's lead article is "Towards a manual for the most common restart" by Ben Bellman, a long throw-ins analysis (source: `home.md`). The kinds of MLS analysis ASA produces — based on the URL slugs we attempted to scrape — include season previews (e.g., 2026 MLS previews mentioning Inter Miami, FC Cincinnati, and Philadelphia), expected-goals leader rankings (`mls-expected-goals-leaders`), academy-system status reports (`state-of-the-mls-academy-system`), and roster-rule explainers (`breaking-down-mls-roster-rules`). The **content** of those analytical pieces, however, was not captured by the scrape.

**MLS Soccer official (`mlssoccer.com`)** provides editorial coverage (news landing, transfer tracker, individual match recaps) and the official standings. The `news.md` snapshot covered Matchday 11 of the 2026 season; recent storylines included Austin FC's prime-time win, Orlando City's comeback over Inter Miami in the Florida Derby, Petar Musa's hat trick for FC Dallas, and Evander's hat trick for FC Cincinnati. The standings page (`standings.md`) reveals the official columns MLS publishes — Rank, Club, Points (PTS), Points Per Game (PPG), Games Played (GP), Wins, Losses, Ties, Goals For, Goals Against, Goal Difference, Home, Away — but the table rows themselves are loaded dynamically via JavaScript and were not captured.
